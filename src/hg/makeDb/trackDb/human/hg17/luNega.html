Description

The potential pseudo fragment track shows genomic fragments that are likely to be pseudogenes. Compared with the homologous known gene in the same genome, the potential pseudo fragment has pseudogene-like features such as frameshift, premature stop codon, non conserved splicing sites, and so on. The structure of a potential pseudo fragment shown in the browser is based on the gene structure of its homologous known gene.

Methods

A set of pre-aligned human known genes is mapped across the human genome through the Human Blastz Self Alignment. We call this method homologous mapping method. The fragments identified by this method are homologues of known genes. The known genes we used are from Consensus CDS (CCDS) gene set, RefSeq gene set, or MGC gene set, which are currently the most reliable gene sets. These genes have been aligned to the human genome and their locations in the genome are available at the UCSC Genome Browser.

We compared each homologous fragment with its known gene and collected a set of features to determine whether the homologous fragment is a gene. The features include sequence identity, dN/dS ratio, the splicing sites, number of premature stop codon, and so on. These homologous fragments either belong to real genes or belong to pseudogenes. We took homologous fragments overlapping known genes as positive samples and overlapping known pseudogenes as negative samples. We used them to train Support Vector Machines (SVMs) to learn how to separate pseudo fragments from pseudo fragments. The trained SVMs are used to classify homologous fragments into potential pseudo elements or potential pseudo elements. Finally, a heuristic filter is used to correct some misclassified fragments and to determine whether the homologous fragment is likely to be a pseudogene.
