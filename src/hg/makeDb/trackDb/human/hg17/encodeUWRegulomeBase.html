Description

This track shows DNase I sensitivity measured across ENOCDE regions using the Quantitative Chromatin Profiling (QCP) method (Dorschner et al (2004). QCP provides a quantitative high-throughout method for the identification of the continous distribution of DNase I sensitivity across gene loci. The moving baseline of mean DNaseI sensitivity is computed over each PCR amplicon using a locally-weighted least squares (LOWESS)-based algorithm. DNaseI has long been used to map general chromatin accessibility and the DNaseI "hyperaccessibility" or "hypersensitivity" that is a universal feature of active cis-regulatory sequences. Use of this method has lead to the discovery of functional regulatory elements that include enhancers, insulators, promotors, locus control regions and novel elements.

Display Conventions and Configuration

DNaseI sensitivity is expressed in standard units, where each increment of 1 unit corresponds to an increase of 1SD from the baseline. The displayed values are calculated as (copies in DNaseI-untreated / copies in DNaseI-treated). Thus, increasing values represent increasing sensitivity. Major DNaseI hypersensitive sites are readily identified as peaks in the signal that exceed 2 SDs (corresponding to the ~95% confidence bound on outliers).

The subtracks within this composite annotation track correspond to data from different tissues, and may be configured in a variety of ways to highlight different aspects of the displayed data. The graphical configuration options are shown at the top of the track description page, followed by a list of subtracks. To display only selected subtracks, uncheck the boxes next to the tracks you wish to hide. For more information about the graphical configuration options, click the Graph configuration help link.

Color differences among the subtracks are arbitrary; they provide a visual cue for distinguishing the different cell lines/phenotypes.

Methods

QCP was performed as described in Dorschner et al. The article describes the methods of chromatin preparation and DNase I digestion. Briefly, DNase I treated nuclei were prepared for each cell type. Primers were designed such that they amplified tiled contiguous amplicons (approximately 250 bp in size) across the study regions. An amplicon tiling path has been computed over all regions and is available through UniSTS. The relative number of intact copies of the genomic DNA sequence was determined using real-time PCR for both DNase-I treated and untreated samples. For the replicates for each amplicon, the mean value of copies remaining in DNaseI-treated vs. untreated samples were compared. The results were analyzed with a statistical algorithm to compute the moving baseline of mean DNase I sensitivity and to identify outliers that correspond with DNase I hypersensitive sites.

Verification

All data points represent the mean of four replicate measurements. Biological replicate sampels were pooled. Validation of the results was carried out by convential DNase I hypersensitivity assays using end-labeling/Southern blotting.
