Description

To obtain a more "genome-centric" view of OMIM and to increase its usefulness to the community, Omicia has mapped OMIM variants onto the human genome sequence as nucleotide changes leading to protein changes. There are 2 subtracks. One has alleles that were mapped in an automated manner, and the other is selected genes where the alleles were hand mapped. The Omicia collection exceeds 2,000 genes with documented associations predisposing to diverse disease phenotypes.

Methods

  • Automated mapping

    Often this is nontrivial since protein sequences derived from the reference human genome differ from those in early publications, or alternatively spliced mRNAs have been annotated onto the genome, leading Omicia to implement sophisticated "shifting algorithms" to resolve discrepancies. Omicia annotes disease-causing intronic variants that may result in cryptic slice sites leading to exon skipping, intron retention, and premature termination events, as well as short deletions and insertions in OMIM. Mutations are scored by how well they overlap with data from other sources, including dbSNP, the Human Genome Mutation Database (HGMD) and SNPs mapped to OMIM by dbSNP.

  • Hand mapping

    Approximately 50 of the genes most accessed at UCSC have been chosen for hand mapping of alleles.
