Description

This track shows a measure of evolutionary conservation in human, rat, and mouse that is based on a phylogenetic hidden Markov model (phylo-HMM). The alignments used were the multiz alignments of the mouse Feb. 2003 (mm3), rat Jun. 2003 (rn3), and human Jul. 2003 (hg16) assemblies.

Methods

A phylo-HMM is a generative probabilistic model that describes both the process of DNA substitution along the branches of a phylogeny at any given site in an alignment, and the potential for the mode of this process to change from one site to the next. Each of the states of a phylo-HMM may be associated with a different phylogenetic model, describing both a set of overall *rates* of substitution along the branches of the phylogeny, and a particular *pattern* of substitution (relative rates for all pairs of nucleotides)

In this case, the pattern of substitution is assumed to be the same for all states, but the overall rates are different. Essentially the same model is assumed as was described by Felsenstein and Churchill (1996). There are k states corresponding to k "rate categories," each of which is defined by a single rate constant. An autocorrelation parameter (lambda) defines the transition probabilities between states. We have estimated the rate constants from the data, as was done by Yang (1995), but have allowed the autocorrelation parameter lambda to be treated as a tuning parameter, which we adjust to get the desired level of "smoothing" in our plots.
