Description

This track shows a measure of evolutionary conservation in $organism, Tetraodon, Fugu, mouse and human based on a phylogenetic hidden Markov model (phastCons). Multiz alignments of the following assemblies were used to generate this annotation:

  • $organism ($date, $db)
  • Tetraodon (Feb. 2004, tetNig1)
  • Fugu (Aug. 2002, fr1)
  • human (Feb. 2006, hg18)
  • mouse (Aug. 2005, mm7)

Display Conventions and Configuration

There are four different display modes for the information in this track: dense, squish, pack, and full. In dense mode, a grayscale gradient is shown in which the darker values represent higher levels of overall conservation as scored by phastCons. In squish, pack, and full modes, the phastCons scores are shown as a wiggle graph where higher conservation scores are shown as taller bars.

In full or pack display mode, the pairwise aliginments of human, mouse, Fugu and Tetraodon to zebrafish are shown. In pack mode, the pairwise alignments are shown in dense display mode using a grayscale density gradient. In full mode, the aligning blocks are shown as wiggles where the height of the bar represents the level of conservation found between zebrafish and the aligned species.

Whenever the pairwise alignments are being displayed, the gaps between the aligned blocks are annotated with information about what is happening in the aligned species in this region. (1) If in the aligned species there are no bases between the aligned blocks, this will be shown as a single line between the aligned blocks. (2) When there are one or more unalignable bases in the aligned species between the bounding aligned regions, two lines are drawn between the aligned blocks. (3) If there are N's in the aligned species between the bounding aligned regions, then the space between the alignment blocks is filled with a yellowish-white color. Evolutionarily, situation (1) suggests either a lineage-specific insertion between the aligned blocks in the zebrafish genome or a lineage-specific deletion between the aligned blocks in the aligned species, situation (2) suggests either excessive evolutionary distance or independent indels in the region between the aligned blocks in both the zebrafish and the aligning species, and situation (3) reflects uncertaintly in the relationship between the zebrafish DNA and the DNA of the aligned species because relevant portions of the aligned species are not yet sequenced.

In addition, the track shows places in the alignment where there is a discontinuity in the location (genomic context) of the aligned DNA in the aligned species. The genomic context can be defined as a chromosome, scaffold or region of the aligned species. Discontinuities are shown either by a vertical blue bar, representing a discontinuity that persists indefinitely on either side (e.g. when DNA for a very long region on either side of the bar comes from a different chromosome in the aligned species due to a large scale rearrangement), or green square brackets, which enclose shorter alignments consisting of DNA from one genomic context in the aligned species that sit inside a larger chain of alignments from a different genomic context. The alignment between the green square brackets might represent a short mis-alignment, a lineage-specific insertion of a transposon in the zebrafish genome that is aligning to a paralogous copy somewhere else in the aligning species, or any number of other similar things.

When zoomed-in to the base-display level, the track shows the base composition of each alignment. The numbers and symbols on the Gaps line indicate the lengths of gaps in the zebrafish sequence at those alignment positions relative to the longest non-zebrafish sequence. If the gap size is greater than 9, a non-numeric symbol is shown: a "*" if the gap size is a multiple of 3; otherwise "+".

Codon translation is also available in base mode if there exists annotation identifying the displayed region as being in a coding segment. There are four different modes controlling how this annotation is displayed. In "No codon translation" mode, the gene annotation is not used so the bases are displayed without translation. In "Use default species for translation" mode, the annotations from the genome displayed in the "Default species for translation" pull-down are used to translate all the aligned species present in the alignment. In "Use frames for species if available, otherwise no translation" mode, codon translation will only be done on those species that are annotated as being coding over the aligned region. In "Use frames for species if available, otherwise use default species" mode, codon translation will be done on those species that are annotated as being coding over the aligned region using their particular species specific annotation while all the rest of the species will be translated using the default species annotation.

To view detailed information about the alignments at a specific position, zoom in the display to 30,000 or fewer bases, then click on the alignment.

The checkboxes in the track configuration section allow the exclusion of species from the pairwise display; however, this does not remove them from the conservation score display.

This track may be configured in a variety of ways to highlight different aspects of the displayed information. Click the "Graph configuration help" link for an explanation of the configuration options.

Methods

Best-in-genome blastz pairwise alignments were multiply aligned using multiz, beginning with $organism-human alignments and subsequently adding in mouse. This was added to an alignment of $organism-Tetraodon aligned to Fugu. The resulting multiple alignments were then assigned conservation scores by phastCons.

The phastCons program computes conservation scores based on a phylo-HMM, a type of probabilistic model that describes both the process of DNA substitution at each site in a genome and the way this process changes from one site to the next (Felsenstein and Churchill 1996, Yang 1995, Siepel and Haussler 2005). PhastCons uses a two-state phylo-HMM, with a state for conserved regions and a state for non-conserved regions. The value plotted at each site is the posterior probability that the corresponding alignment column was "generated" by the conserved state of the phylo-HMM. These scores reflect the phylogeny (including branch lengths) of the species in question, a continuous-time Markov model of the nucleotide substitution process, and a tendency for conservation levels to be autocorrelated along the genome (i.e., to be similar at adjacent sites). The general reversible (REV) substitution model was used. Note that, unlike many conservation-scoring programs, phastCons does not rely on a sliding window of fixed size, so short highly-conserved regions and long moderately conserved regions can both obtain high scores. More information about phastCons can be found in Siepel et al. (2005).

PhastCons currently treats alignment gaps as missing data, which sometimes has the effect of producing undesirably high conservation scores in gappy regions of the alignment. We are looking at several possible ways of improving the handling of alignment gaps.
