  What does it do?
 

As vertebrate genome sequences near completion and research re-focuses on their analysis, the issue of effective sequence display becomes critical: it is not helpful to have 3 billion letters of genomic DNA shown as plain text. As an alternative, the UCSC Genome Browser provides a rapid and reliable display of any requested portion of genomes at any scale, together with dozens of aligned annotation tracks (known genes, predicted genes, ESTs, mRNAs, CpG islands, assembly gaps and coverage, chromosomal bands, mouse homologies, and more).

Half of the annotation tracks are computed at UCSC from publicly available sequence data. The remaining tracks are provided by collaborators worldwide. Users can also add their own custom tracks to the browser for educational or research purposes.

The genome browser stacks annotation tracks beneath genome coordinate positions, allowing rapid visual correlation of different types of information. The user can look at a whole chromosome to get a feel for gene density, open a specific cytogenetic band to see a positionally mapped disease gene candidate, or zoom in to a particular gene to view its spliced ESTs and possible alternative splicing. The browser itself does not draw conclusions; rather, it collates all relevant information in one location, leaving the exploration and interpretation to the user.

The browser supports text and sequence based searches that provide quick, precise access to any region of specific interest. Secondary links from individual entries within annotation tracks lead to sequence details and supplementary off-site databases.

To control information overload, tracks need not be displayed in full. Options allow hidden, flattened (dense), or filtered displays according to user criteria. An individual track will not display more than 300 items, reverting to dense mode despite a controller set at full. Zooming and scrolling controls also help narrow or broaden the chromosomal range displayed to just the desired region.

A blue navigation bar link at the top allows convenient capture of raw genomic DNA sequence for the coordinate range displayed in the browser window. This DNA can encode track features via elaborate text formatting options. Other links tie the genome browser to the BLAT alignment tool, provide table browser access to the underlying relational database, convert coordinates across different assembly dates, and open the window at the complementary Ensembl annotation.

Clicking on an individual item in a track opens its details page with summaries of feature properties and links to off-site repositories such as PubMed, GenBank, LocusLink, and OMIM. Position, cytoband, strand, data source, and any encoded protein, mRNA, genomic sequence and alignment are furnished as appropriate to the nature of the track.

The browser data represents an immense collaborative effort involving thousands of people from the international biomedical research community. UCSC itself does no sequencing and creates only about half of the tracks in-house; its other major roles include building genome assemblies, creating the genome browser work environment, and serving it all online. Sequence data, annotation tracks, and even software are in the public domain (with rare caveats) and are available for anyone to download.



  Getting started: browser gateways
 

To get oriented, it is best to jump to a gene or region of the genome that you are already familiar with. To do this, choose an assembly date from the main page, locate the position text box in the gateway page, and enter a gene symbol, mRNA or EST accession number, chromosome band, descriptive term likely to occur in GenBank text, or a specific chromosomal range (e.g., chr20:1-25000), or simply accept the default. Proceed to the active track browser window and experiment for 15 minutes with the scroll adjustments, window width, track controllers, and auxiliary links.

The genome browser sometimes returns several matches in response to a position search instead of an active window. Clicking on any item opens the track window to the indicated segment of the genome. Base position numbering begins at the telomere of short p arm at the far left. A track may be hidden, projected, displayed selectively according to a filter, or displayed in full according to how its controller is set.

Once the browser is correctly displaying the region of interest, save the coordinate range (or bookmark the page) if you plan to return to that view or share it, remembering that individual preference settings may vary. Coordinates of a given feature on an unfinished chromosome will change with each genome assembly because of filled gaps, fewer artefactual duplications, and corrected strand orientations. Coordinates thus become obsolete; it is usually best to work with the most recent assembly even though a full set of tracks might not yet be ready. Note however the browser offers a tool that can correctly convert coordinates between different assembly dates.

The keyword gateway doesn't always find its query. It is not a site-wide search engine but instead derives primarily from GenBank mRNA records whose text annotations can include gene names, gene symbols, journal title words, author names, and RefSeq mRNAs (NM series). Queries such as post-freeze GenBank entries, withdrawn gene names, abandoned synonyms, and genomic intervals (e.g., NT contigs) will not work. UniGene and NP protein accession numbers will shortly be accepted as queries. LocusLink and OMIM accessions are difficult to support being plain 4 to 6 digit numbers lacking diagnostic letters; however OMIM links to LocusLink which now carries map links to the UCSC browser. Additional text can be found in detail pages for individual features and in searchable non-positional tables such as refLink (see below).

If you have genomic, mRNA, or protein sequence but no name or no idea where it maps in the genome, the BLAT gateway will rapidly locate it by homology alignment, provided the region has been sequenced. Close members of the gene family will be found at the same time, as well as assembly duplication artefacts. A whole set of query sequences can be looked up at once when provided in fasta format. In BLAT output, first look at details of the alignment and then proceed into the browser itself if warranted by match quality. When too many hits occur, try resubmitting the query sequence after filtering in slow mode at RepeatMasker.

The table browser, a friendly portal to the underlying open source MySQL relational database driving the genome browser, allows genomic data to be viewed as columns of text rather than as graphical tracks. Select a positional table for any track and enter a chromosomal range (enter "genome" to get all). Choose fields to limit the columns displayed; get DNA to obtain fasta sequences. Positional tables have chrom, chromStart, and chromEnd fields that coalesce to a genome browser gateway position (e.g. chrX:12345-67890). Non-positional tables offer various kinds of accessory information needed to build the detail pages. The genome and table browsers are necessarily in synchrony with each other being just different views of the same fundamental MySQL database they reference. Technical explanations of individual fields are provided onsite but opening up that column usually offers adequate clues.

External gateways can also provide direct links into the UCSC genome browser, for example LocusLink, AceView, Ensembl, SuperFamily, GeneLynx, and GeneCards. Journal articles can also link to the browser and provide custom tracks; be sure to use the assembly date appropriate to the provided coordinates.

To insure uninterrupted browser services for your research during UCSC server maintenance and power outages, bookmark a geographically convenient mirror site that replicates the UCSC genome browser.

Please bear in mind that the genome browser cannot outperform the underlying quality of the draft genome -- assembly errors and sequence gaps may still occur well into 2003 due to intrinsically difficult-to-sequence regions. Artifactual duplications arise as unavoidable compromises during a build, causing misleading matches in genome coordinates found by alignment. Post-freeze GenBank submissions lead to gradual track obsolescence (which can be addressed by supplemental BLAST searches). The genome browser does the best it can to operate under this environment.



  Fine-tuning the track display
 

The browser track window opens to default values that may need adjustment. First set the pixel width to take advantage of your monitor. For example, setting a 19" monitor to 1100 pixels makes more of the genome visible without redraws. The maximum width supported is 5000 pixels but it is best not to exceed your monitor width initially. Adjust the track controllers according to your interest in individual tracks. The browser can remember many user preferences from session to session (however it never monitors user activities or records submitted data). Use the reset button periodically to restore default settings.

Tracks offer considerable control over what is displayed:
--If "hide" is chosen, the track will not be displayed at all. Someone not interested in SNPs or mouse synteny might want these tracks hidden to reduce track clutter and improve speed. Some tracks may pertain to only one chromosome (e.g., the Sanger annotation of chromosome 20); no controller will display when other chromosomes are in the browser.
--If "dense" is chosen, the track will display all relevant features condensed (projected) to a single line. To get a feel for known gene density without excessive detail, open a whole chromosome and set the known gene track to dense.
--If "full" is chosen, the track will display a separate line for each constituent feature. Use this option sparingly as hundreds of ESTs might align with a given gene. When the number of requested tracks exceeds 300, the browser defaults to dense mode. Narrowing the chromosomal range or using a track filter (e.g., restricting the EST track to a specified tissue or library) reduces the number of items displayed, re-enabling full display.

Track filters are accessed from linked text above each controller underneath the main graphic or from the mini-buttons to the left of the track in the main graphic. Depending on the track, this new feature allows users to color a subset of individual items in a track and display them at the top of the stack. This is helpful when too many items occur, especially when only some are relevant to the current task. For instance to highilight ESTs expressed in the liver, put 'liver' into tissue control of the EST filters page. Consult entries in the table browser for permissible filter word values. For example the non-positional table "library" shows "NIH_MGC_67 " is an option for an EST filter. You can use wildcards in filters as well. "NIH_MGC*" would highlight all ESTs generated by the Mammalian Gene Collection project. In the filter screen there is also a description of the track, the method used in making it, and credit its source. Filters, like all user interface settings, are now persistent from session to session on the same web browser. Either delete all the text in the filter controls or use the 'reset all' button to remove filters.

Usually the amount of flanking region needs adjustment. Zoom in and out using the 1.5, 3 and 10-fold buttons available at the top of the browser, or scroll sideways by 10%, 50%, or 95% of the displayed size (given in base pairs). It is also possible to hold one edge fixed and adjust the other by any multiple of the vertical blue line interval. Another option is to manually adjust position range numbers and refresh the display with the jump button.

The browser graphic responds to mouse clicks on tracks and labels:
--If the base position track is clicked, the display zooms in 3-fold, re-centered on the coordinate of the click.
--If the click is on the optional explanatory label in the center of the display or left panel, the track toggles between full and dense mode.
--If the track is already in full mode, clicking on a specific feature opens a details window with name, sequence, and off-site links.

At 1 pixel per base pair scale, the browser display of genes will accurately provide exon and intron width and direction of transcription by hatches for multi-exon features (consult the details page for strand of single exon genes). At larger scale, thin exons would soon disappear unless favored; this comes at the expense of intron width and overall display fidelity -- an exon may falsely appear to fall within a RepeatMasker feature at some scales. The browser seeks to maintain a useful view even at scales where a whole chromosome is shown in a 610 pixel window.

The genome browser can open from a lab web site or journal article link to preferred defaults to provide a consistent user experience. This also applies to custom tracks. A simpler static alternative is to save the browser window as a gif graphic. This can be done as a screen shot to embed chromosomal range, size, and scale. Browser text in the left panel is already rendered as image, meaning it cannot be copied into a text editor. However text can be extracted from "source" html or more conveniently from the table browser.



  Details on individual tracks
  Remember:
-- Credit goes to many individuals and institutions for generously contributing tracks. For specific information about contributors to a given track, look at the track's description page.
-- A track does not appear if its display mode is set to hide.
-- If you are not interested in technical assembly details, hide the associated tracks.
-- A track set to full display mode will display as dense if total tracks exceed 300.
-- Clicking on a track's center label toggles between full and dense display modes.
-- Clicking on a track feature or name when the track is in full display mode will show the details page for the track.
-- Only the most recent assemblies are fully active. Older assemblies are archived.
-- Not all tracks appear in all assemblies. Only basic tracks appear initially in a new assembly.
-- Track data can be viewed equivalently in columns as text tables. Individual field names (columns) are explained elsewhere.
-- The tracks briefly described below are those found in recent assemblies. Additional information can often be found on the track description pages.

Acembly Genes
- This track shows gene models reconstructed solely from mRNA and EST evidence by Danielle and Jean Thierry-Mieg and Vahan Simonyan using the Acembly program. Acembly attempts to find the best alignment of each mRNA against the genome, and considers alternative splice models. If more than one gene model is produced that has statistical significance, all of these models are displayed.
- The display conventions for this track are the same as those for the Known Genes track.
- Clicking on an entry in full display mode will show position information, links to translated protein, predicted mRNA, and genomic sequence, and a link to an extensive text and graphical annotation of supporting data at NCBI's AceView.
- To view a text version of the data set, choose the acembly position table in the Table Browser.

Assembly
- This track shows the draft assembly of the human genome. This assembly merges contigs from overlapping draft and finished clones into longer sequence contigs. The sequence contigs are ordered and oriented when possible by mRNA, EST, paired plasmid reads (from the SNP Consortium) and BAC end sequence pairs.
- In dense display mode, this track depicts in alternating gold and brown the path through the draft and finished clones (aka the golden path) used to create the assembled sequence. Individual boxes show the assembly extractable from a single clone fragment. Where gaps exist in the path, spaces are shown between the gold and brown blocks. If the relative order and orientation of the contigs between the two blocks is known, a line is drawn to bridge the blocks. The display must be sufficiently zoomed in to view the gap and bridge features.
- Clicking on an item in full display mode will show information about the clone fragment and bases, position information, and the DNA sequence corresponding to the fragment.
- To view a text version of the data set, choose the chrN_gold position table in the Table Browser.

BAC End Pairs
- This track shows the genome sequence of bacterial artificial chromosomes (BACs) in which a small amount of the both ends have been sequenced to place the full insert on the assembly. A valid pair of BAC end sequences must be separated by at least 50Kb but no more than 600Kb, and must have opposite orientations. Strand assignment follows Clone Registry storage conventions. The BAC end sequences are placed on the assembled sequence using BLAT.
- Clicking on an entry in full display mode will show hyperlinks to the NCBI clone registry and GenBank, position information, and genomic alignments.
- To view a text version of the data set, choose the bacEndPairs position table in the Table Browser.

Base Position
- Shows the chromosomal coordinates as numbered from the telomere of the short p arm. The coordinates differ between assemblies.
- Clicking on a position in this track will zoom in the display by 3x, centered around the click.
- This track has only on and off modes, no dense mode.

Chromosome Band
- The light and dark blocks indicate the approximate location of traditional cytological bands seen on Giemsa-stained chromosomes at an 850 band resolution. The bands were mapped by fluorescent in situ hybridization (FISH) of large-insert clones with sequence tags, STS markers, and BAC end sequence locations. resolution of 862 bands genome-wide. The uncertainty of mapping to band staining is still a megabase or more.
- Clicking on a band in full display mode will show more details about the track, a discussion of mapping methods used, and lab credits.
- To view a text version of the data set, choose the cytoBand position table in the Table Browser.

Coverage
- In dense display mode, this track shows the coverage level of the genome. Finished regions are shown in black. Draft regions are shown in various shades of gray that correspond to the level of coverage:
o White: no coverage (gap)
o Light Gray: predraft (less than 4x shotgun)
o Medium Gray: draft (at least 4x shotgun)
o Dark Gray: covered by multiple draft clones
o Black: covered by a finished clone
- In full display mode, coverage for each unfinished clone is represented as a two-tiered gray box. The fragments inside the clone are shown as numbered black boxes. Their positions correspond to their relative assembly placement, including strand assignment. For some assemblies, clones in the sequencing center tiling path are shown with blue rather than gray backgrounds.
- Clicking on an item in full display mode will show the clone hyperlink to Genbank record and the fasta format sequence (one per fragment).
- To view a text version of the data set, choose the clonePos position table in the Table Browser.

CpG Islands
- This track shows CpG islands, areas rich in the dinucleotide CG. A CpG island is a region where C's followed by G's (CpGs) are present at significantly higher levels than is typical for the genome as a whole. The CpG islands displayed in this browser are all at least 200 bases long, and have a GC content of at least 50%.
- CpG islands are associated with genes, particularly housekeeping genes, in vertebrates. They are particularly common near transcription start sites, and may be associated with promoter regions. Normally a CpG is rare in vertebrate DNA because the C's in such an arrangement tend to be methylated. This methylation helps distinguish the newly synthesized DNA strand from the parent strand, which aids in the final stages of DNA proofreading after duplication. However, over evolutionary time, methylated C's tend to turn into T's due to spontaneous deamination. The result is that CpGs are relatively rare unless there is selective pressure to keep them or a region is not methylated for some reason, perhaps having to do with the regulation of gene expression.
- Dark green indicates CpG islands that exceed 400 occurrences. Clicking in a CpG entry in full display mode will show basic track information, detailed information about the CpG island, and sequence information.
- To view a text version of the data set, choose the cpgIsland positional table in the Table Browser.

Duplications
- This track shows genomic duplications greater than 1,000 bp if not overly repeat-masked: as red if 99%+ identity (likely unmerged assembly overlap artifact), as yellow for 98-99% identity (origin uncertain), and as gray for 90-98% identity (below draft sequencing error, often genuine pericentromeric or subtelomeric duplication). Duplications are more common in unordered and unassigned contigs (i.e., difficult to assign to their proper position).
- In the left panel of the browser, duplications are named by the chromosome and start of the other member of the duplication pair. Clicking on an individual duplication gives details of both locations and match quality parameters.

Ensembl Genes
- This track shows gene predictions from Project Ensembl. For a description of the methods used in Ensembl gene prediction, refer to The Ensembl genome database project, Nucleic Acids Research, 2002, 30(1) 38-41.
- The display conventions for this track are the same as those for the Known Genes track.
- Clicking on an entry in full display mode brings up basic track information, links to the translated protein, predicted mRNA, and genomic sequences, plus a link to detailed supporting evidence.
- To view a text version of the data set, choose the ensGene position table in the Table Browser.

Exofish ecores
- The track displays conserved regions, generally protein-coding homologs, from a genomics project of long random reads in the pufferfish, Tetraodon nigroviridis. Regions of greater homology are displayed in a darker blue. Ecore is an acronym for evolutionary conserved region; the data was used to estimate the number of human genes.
- Clicking in full mode brings up information on a particular ecore, access the relevant Genoscope page, and the relevant DNA.

Fgenesh++ Genes
- This tracks shows Fgenesh++ gene predictions based on Softberry Inc.'sgene-finding software. Fgenesh++ uses both hidden Markov models (HMMs) and protein similarity to find genes in a completely automated manner, with less emphasis on cDNA/EST data. See the paper "Ab initio gene finding in Drosophila genomic DNA", Genome Research 10(4) 516-522 for more information.
- The display conventions for this track are the same as those for the Known Genes track.
- Clicking on an entry in full display mode brings up basic track information, links to the translated protein, predicted mRNA, and genomic sequences, plus a link to the best matching protein at GenBank.
- To view a text version of the human data set, choose the softberryGene position table in the Table Browser.
- To view a text version of the mouse data set, choose the softberryMouse position table in the Table Browser.

Fish Blat
- This track displays translated alignments of 728 million bases of Tetraodon nigrovirisi whole genome shotgun reads vs. the draft human genome. Areas painted by this track are quite likely to be coding regions. The alignments were done with BLAT in translated protein mode requiring 2 nearby 4-mer matches to trigger a detailed alignment. The human genome was masked with RepeatMasker and Tandem Repeat Finder before running BLAT. The Tetraodon sequence was provided by Genoscope.
- Clicking on an entry in full display mode brings up links to the DNA sequence and the alignment.
- To view a text version of the data set, choose the chrN_blatFish position table in the Table Browser.

FISH Clones
- Shows the location of fluorescent in situ hybridization (FISH) mapped clones along the draft assembly sequence.
- Clicking on a specific marker in full display mode will show information on clone placement, clone registry, its STS markers, and BAC end sequences.
- To view a text version of the data set, choose the fishClones position table in the Table Browser.

Gap
- This track depicts gaps in the assembly. These gaps - with the exception of intractable heterochromatic gaps - will be closed during the finishing process.
- Gaps are represented as black boxes. If the relative order and orientation of the contigs on either side of the gap is known from mRNA, ESTs, or paired BAC end reads, it is a "bridged" gap and a white line is drawn through the black box representing the gap. The display must be sufficiently zoomed in to view these features: at a coarser scale, the gaps can appear to coalesce in the the graphic. In full display mode, the gap label indicates the type of gap (see below) and whether the gap is bridged.
- Strand misorientation errors can occur adjacent to unbridged gaps. Unbridged gaps are assigned somewhat arbitrary sequence sizes (runs of N's in genomic DNA); uncertainty is less with bridged gaps. A gap within contig fragments of a clone is typically smaller than a gap between draft clones.
- There are four principle types of gaps:
o Fragment: gaps between the contigs of a draft clone. These are represented by 100 N's in the assembly, whether or not there is bridging information.
o Clone: gaps between clones in the same map contig. These are represented by 50,000 N's in the assembly if the gap is bridged or 100,000 N's if it is not.
o Contig - gaps between map contigs. These are represented by 200,000 N's in the assembly.
o Heterochromatin - gaps from centromeres (3,000,000 N's) or other large blocks of heterochromatin (size varies).
- Clicking on an item in full display mode will show the gap type, bridging information and position information about the gap. Unfinished clones may have gaps of uncertain size between fragments.
- To view a text version of the data set, choose the chrN_gap position table in the Table Browser.

GC Percent
- This shows the percentage of bases that are G or C in a 20,000 base window. Windows with high GC content are drawn more darkly than those with low GC content. High GC content is associated with gene rich areas. The average GC percent across the human genome is 41%. Both Celera and public consortium reported isochore concept in conflict with human genome sequence data.
- Clicking in full mode provides position information.
- To view a text version of the data set, choose the gcPercent position table in the Table Browser.

Geneid Gene Predictions
- This track shows gene predictions from the geneid program, which is being developed at the Grup de Recerca en Informatica Biomedica at IMIM in Barcelona. Geneid uses information from sequence signals involved in genes specification, as well as coding coding statistics, to define exons and genes.
- The display conventions for this track are the same as those for the Known Genes track.
- Clicking on an entry in full display mode brings up basic track information, plus links to the translated protein, predicted mRNA, and genomic sequences.
- To view a text version of the data set, choose the geneid position table in the Table Browser.

Genscan Genes
- This track shows gene predictions from GenScan. These predictions are based on transcriptional, translational, and donor and acceptor splicing signals, plus length and compositional distributions of exons, introns and intergenic regions. The program does not use similarity to known proteins like GenomeScan. For a description of the Genscan program and the model that underlies it, refer to Burge, C. and Karlin, S. (1997) Prediction of complete gene structures in human genomic DNA. J. Mol. Biol. 268, 78-94.
- The display conventions for this track are the same as those for the Known Genes track.
- Clicking on an entry in full display mode will show basic track information, plus links to the translated protein, predicted mRNA, and genomic sequences.
- To view a text version of the data set, choose the genscan position table in the Table Browser.

GNF Ratio
- This track shows expression data from GNF (The Genomics Institute of the Novartis Research Foundation) using Affymetrix GeneChips. For more information about the experiments, refer to the description page for this track.
- In dense display mode, the track color denotes the average signal over all experiments on a log base 2 scale. Lighter colors correspond to lower signals and darker colors correspond to higher signals.
- In full display mode, the color of each item represents the log base 2 ratio of the signal of that particular experiment to the median signal of all experiments for that probe.
- Clicking on the mini-button to the left of the track will display the track description page that contains a filter to fine-tune the track display. The filter allows the user to change the display mode, group the displayed results, and change the display colors.
- To view a text version of the data set, choose the affyRatio position table in the Table Browser.

Human ESTs
- This track shows alignments between Expressed Sequence Tags (ESTs) - spliced and unspliced - in Genbank and the genome. ESTs are single read (typically approximately 500 base) sequences that usually represent fragments of transcribed genes. To view only spliced ESTs, see the Spliced EST track.
- This track was generated by aligning human ESTs from Genbank against the genome using the BLAT program. Note that the maximum intron length allowed by BLAT is 500,000 bases, which may eliminate some ESTs with very long introns that might otherwise align. When a single EST aligns in multiple places, the alignment having the highest base identity is found. Only alignments that have a base identity level within 1% of the best are kept. Alignments must also have at least 93% base identity to be kept.
- Aligning regions (usually exons) are shown as black boxes connected by lines for gaps (usually spliced out introns). In dense display mode, the degree of darkeness corresponds to the number of spliced ESTs aligning to the region.
- In full display mode, only 300 track entries can be displayed in the browser at a time. If you've set the track display mode to full, but the track continues to display in dense mode, you will need to restrict the scope by zooming in to a smaller region. Hatch marks on the introns indicate the direction of transcription. In the December 2001 assembly and later, this direction is taken by looking at the splice sites. In previous assemblies, the direction of transcription was taken from the Genbank annotations, which frequently were inaccurate; these should be verified in the details page alignment on the basis of the GT-AG splice donor/acceptor rule.
- Clicking on the mini-button to the left of the track will display the track description page which contains a filter to fine-tune the track display. The filter allows the user to restrict the display to only those entries that match certain keywords, and to change the color of the selected entries.
- Clicking on an individual EST entry will show basic track information, the accession number, GenBank highlights, and links to EST/Genomic Alignments.
- To view a text version of the data set, choose the chrN_est positional table in the Table Browser.

Human mRNAs
- The Human mRNA track shows alignments between human mRNAs in Genbank and the genome. Genbank human mRNAs are aligned against the genome using the BLAT program. When a single mRNA aligns in multiple places, the alignment having the highest base identity is found. Only alignments that have a base identity level within 1% of the best are kept. Alignments must also have at least 95% base identity to be kept.
- Aligning regions (usually exons) are shown as black boxes connected by lines for gaps (usually spliced out introns). In dense display mode, darker gray items correspond to better quality matches. In full display mode, hatch marks on the introns indicate the direction of transcription.
- Clicking on the mini-button to the left of the track will display the track description page which contains a filter to fine-tune the track display. The filter allows the user to restrict the display to only those entries that match certain keywords, and to change the color of the selected entries.
- Clicking on an entry in full display mode will show a summary of the Genbank entry on that mRNA, convenient hyperlinks to major databases, and base-by-base mRNA/genomic alignments.
- To view a text version of the data set, choose the chrN_mrna positional table in the Table Browser.

Known Genes
- The Known Gene track shows known protein coding genes taken from mRNA reference sequences compiled at LocusLink. Coding exons are displayed taller than 5' and 3' untranslated regions (UTRs). Connecting introns are one-pixel lines with hatch marks indicating direction of transcription. Connecting introns are one-pixel lines with hatch marks indicating the direction of transcription. Non-coding RNA genes have their own track in some assemblies.

Map Contigs
- This track shows the locations of contigs of clones on the physical map.
- In assembly versions prior to the August 6 2001 freeze, this track was based on the Washington University accession map, which in turn was based on a fingerprint contig (FPC) map described in 'A physical map of the human genome' in Nature volume 409 pages 934-941.
- Starting with the August 6 2001 freeze, this track is based on tiling path fingerprint (TPF) maps curated by the sequencing centers responsible for each chromosome. The TPF maps are merged with the FPC map, favoring the TPF map where conflicts occur. This step increases the clone coverage substantially over that in the TPF maps. The clone contigs in this merged map are shown in this track.
- Clicking on an item in full display mode will show a brief description of the extent and number of clones in contig, and the DNA sequence.
- To view a text version of the data set, choose the ctgPos position table in the Table Browser.

Mouse Blat
- This track displays alignments of mouse genome to human. The mouse genomic DNA is from the Arachne assembly of mouse whole genome shotgun reads available at the Trace Repository. The alignments were done with BLAT in translated protein mode requiring 2 nearby 4-mer matches to trigger a detailed alignment. The human genome was masked with RepeatMasker and Tandem Repeat Finder prior to running BLAT. Regions with more than 300 aligned reads were assumed novel repeat elements and filtered out. Alignments of greater than 97% identity may represent mouse contamination of human or vice versa.

Mouse ESTs

  BLAT Alignments
  To locate a nucleotide or protein within the human genome, go to the BLAT search page, paste in a sequence or fasta formatted list (header lines start with > and contain a unique name), select the preferred assembly date, sort order, and output option. Numbers, spaces, and extraneous characters are ignored. Alternatively, upload an unformatted plain text file containing the sequences from your desktop using the file browse button.

A single DNA sequence over 20,000 bp will be rejected, but with multiple sequences 50,000 bp is acceptable (e.g., 25 sequences of length 2,000). For protein, these limits are 4,000 and 12,500 amino acids, respectively.

For a DNA query, BLAT quickly finds sequences with 95% or greater similarity over 40 bp or more. BLAT may not locate more diverged or shorter genomic sequences, though perfect sequence matches of 33 bp are found and sometimes as few as 22 bp. Large inserts (e.g., introns) do not throw off alignment. A primate query will generally work but more remote tetrapod DNA will often be too distant.

For a protein query, BLAT rapidly locates genomic sequences with 80% or greater similarity over 20 amino acids or more. This means gene family members that arose within the last 350 million years can generally be detected. More diverged sequences can be aligned to human genome using NCBI's BLAST and psi-BLAST followed by BLAT of the resulting match into the UCSC genome assembly.

A single query, whether DNA or protein, can generate multiple hits. This happens when the genome contains multiple copies, paralogs, pseudogenes, statistical coincidences, artifactual assembly duplications, or when the query itself contains repeats or common retrotransposons. When several nearby BLAT matches occur on a single chromosome, a simple trick can quickly adjust the genome browser view to display them all: open the match having the smallest initial coordinate, then paste in the most distant coordinate from the BLAT output page and refresh the browser view.

Common uses of BLAT are finding genomic coordinates of a mRNA or protein within the current assembly, determining exon structure of a gene, displaying a coding region within a full length gene, isolating an EST of special interest as its own track, searching for gene family members, and finding human homologs of a query from another species. Set the sort order option at the time of query submission to reflect your research interests. The highest scoring output line is the closest in sequence to the query. A BLAT track can be added at any time from within a genome browser view or used simultaneously with custom tracks.

To make a custom track directly from BLAT, select the PSL format output option. The custom track can be launched by pasting it into the bottom of the main browser gateway page.

Ordinary BLAT output is a flat database file that summarizes any alignments found and provides further links to sequence alignment details and the appropriate genome browser view. The details link gives the letter-by-letter alignment to genomic. The score is figured as matches - mismatches in the final alignment of query to genome. Details of BLAT's internal scoring schemes and its overall n-mer alignment seed strategy will be described shortly in a paper by WJ Kent.

BLAT is not BLAST. With DNA, BLAT uses an index of the entire genome kept in memory. The index consists of all non-overlapping 11-mers except for those heavily associated with repeats. The index occupies a gigabyte of RAM. Once index matches are found, corresponding regions of genomic sequence are then loaded into memory for a detailed alignment. Thus the genome itself need not be kept in memory. With protein queries, BLAT works similarly but with 4-mers of genome translated in all frames. The protein index for a mammalian genome requires 2.5 gigabytes of RAM and a separate server.

The BLAT servers are mirrored at several institutions to ensure continuous availability. For large batch jobs and internal parameter changes, it is best to install command line BLAT on your own Linux server. Sources and executables are free for academic, personal, and non-profit purposes. Non-exclusive commercial licenses are also available; contact Jim Kent.



  Getting started on the Table Browser
  The table browser allows convenient and precise access to the primary database text tables containing the human genome sequence and associated annotation tracks. By specifying chromosomal range, table type, and desired columns, the exact data set of interest can be displayed without information overload. This is equivalent to but far more convenient than downloading and manipulating the whole human genome file with its multi-gigabyte data tracks (though that option always remains available.)

After each round of genome assembly, features such as mRNAs are located within the genome by BLAT alignment. This process generates positional stop-start coordinates and other descriptive data which are then stored within MySQL relational database tables. It is these tables that drive the graphical tracks in the Genome Browser, meaning that the two views of the data, tabular or graphic, are always in agreement (a week's lag can occur in downloadable files). Chromosomal coordinates usually change with each build because of newly filled gaps or assembly procedure refinements.

Other data is inherently non-positional (not tied to genome coordinates), such as submitting author of a GenBank EST, tissue of origin , or link to a RefSeq. These data types are accessed separately.

Familiarize yourself with the table browser tool by a few minutes of exploration. Starting at the top of the form, use the default position (or enter a cytological band, e.g. 20p12, or type in a keyword from a GenBank entry to look up). Notice that if you look up a gene, the tool can return a choice of several mRNAs. Clicking on one automatically enters the chromosomal range in the text box.Next select a positional table from the menu, for example, chrN_mrna which holds mRNA alignment data broken down by individual chromosome. Now choose the fields you wish to view, or bypass this step to see all, or view a fasta list of relevant sequences without any table attributes other than chromosome, freeze date, and start-stop genomic coordinates. If the chromosomal range is too narrow, it may happen that no mRNA occurs there (meaning no data will be returned).

Users not already familiar with what the table offers should select "Choose fields". Some tables offer many columns of data (explained elsewhere) of which only a few might be relevant. The fewer fields selected, the simpler the next stage of data return. UCSC plans to add record filtering shortly. This will reduce the size of huge tables by allowing both row and column selection.

Limited boolean logic processing (e.g., joins of two tracks) may be offered in the future. For now, post-processing of table browser output can be done in the user's spreadsheet or database. These steps can produce output in the format needed for custom tracks.



  Track-formatted DNA
 

This aspect of the browser allows features to be displayed via text formatting of the DNA underlying a browser window. It is accessed from the navigation bar at the top of the main browser or from appropriate detail pages. Formatting can range from simply putting exons in upper case to elaborately mark-up of sequence according to multiple track data -- DNA covered by various tracks can be highlighted by case, underlining, bold, italic, and color.

Color values range from 0 (darkest) to 255 (lightest) and are additive (red and green make yellow etc.). The examples below show a few ways to highlight individual tracks and their interplay. Keep it simple at first -- it is easy to make pretty but completely cryptic displays.

--To put exons from the Known Genes track in upper case red text, check the appropriate box in the Toggle Case column and set the color to pure red, RGB (255,0,0). Upon submitting, any known gene within the designated chromosomal interval will appear in red capital letters.
--To see the overlap between Known Genes and Genscan predictions, try setting Known Genes to red (255,0,0) and Genscan to green (0,255,0). Places where the Known Genes and Genscan overlap will be painted yellow (255,255,0).
--To get a level-of-coverage effect for tracks like Spliced ESTs with many overlapping features in full mode, initially select a darker color such as deep green, RGB (0,64,0). Nucleotides covered by a single EST will appear dark green, while regions covered with more ESTs get progressively brighter -- saturating at 4 ESTs.
--To use a track to mask unwanted features, for example set the RepeatMasker track to RGB (255,255,255) to white-out Genscan predictions of LINEs that are not mainstream host genes. Similarly, masking with Known Genes will show what is new in the gene prediction sector.

The default line width of 60 characters is conventional; set this higher to see more DNA at once with fewer line breaks if you plan on locating specific sequences with your web browser search tool.

Be careful about requesting complex formatting for very large chromosomal regions. After all the html tags are added to the output page, the file size may exceed size limits that your browser, clipboard, and other software can safely display. The tool will format about 10 Mbp.

Now copying and pasting the web page output to a text editor such as Word will retain upper case but lose colors and other formatting. That's still useful because other web tools such as BLAST can be set to ignore lower case. To fully capture formatting such as color and underlining, view the output as "source" html in your web browser, copy the output page into an html editor, and display on a web page. It is also possible via selective deletion to process formatted text into a Blat query that will result in a custom track.

The items in the track column usable for masking are auto-generated and so correctly correspond to browser tracks currently offered for particular assembly dates. Formatting is available on all actively supported assemblies.

Further features under consideration include a menu of pre-cooked palettes that do common tasks effectively (to avoid re-entry of a successful but complex coding scheme) and a legend gif on the output page displaying track formatting chosen.



  Custom Tracks
 

Custom tracks are a very important research feature of the browser. Because space on the main genome browser is very tight, some excellent potential genome-wide tracks cannot be offered. Researchers also develop track data for a portion of a chromosome that might not be of general interest or that cannot be shared until journal publication.

Custom tracks work very similarly to ordinary tracks, except that they never become part of the MySQL genome database. They have their own controller and persist as long as their launching window is kept open, even if some portion of the genome is displayed where they are not represented. Custom tracks are normally aligned under genomic sequence but they can also be completely unrelated to human data and run under strings of millions of N's that occur in some assemblies.

Custom tracks are made in one of four formats adhered to fairly strictly. The easiest way to make correctly formatted custom tracks is to collect Blat PSL output. Advanced users can make custom tracks from the table browser and track-formatted DNA. To make a custom track from scratch, begin with the examples and experiment with new lines and altered values in a spreadsheet or text editor.

A custom track can be launched by pasting or uploading it into the bottom of the main browser gateway page. An external custom track (a correctly formatted plain text web page) can be included by adding a separate line starting with http:// Custom tracks can appear in conjunction with ordinary Blat tracks.

After getting your data to display within the UCSC genome browser, you may wish to create your own detail pages from individual track items. This can be done using an attribute of the Track Lines formatting instructions: "url - A URL for an external link associated with this track. Any '$$' in this string this will be substituted with the item name." This creates a chimeric browser, with browser display at UCSC but details at the user's site (or some other database). All the detail pages can reside in a single document if the name denotes an html anchor (e.g., #name).

To share your custom tracks with the biomedical research community, construct a link from your own web pages to the browser based on this example. The position variable tells the browser which part of the genome to display, here chr22:1-20000. The db variable provides the freeze number with 'hg8' corresponding to the August 2001 assembly (only the last 3 are supported). The customText variable refers to any web URL, in the example a UCSC page containing a custom track as plain text in one of the allowed formats. Users normally would change this link to their own track data site.

http://genome.ucsc.edu/cgi-bin/hgTracks?position=chr22:1-20000
&db=hg8&hgt.customText=http://genome.cse.ucsc.edu/test.bed

Please send in links to tracks you'd like to share, along with a one paragraph description of the track and the genome version it is tied to. UCSC plans to create a gallery of examples of supplemental and interesting custom tracks.
