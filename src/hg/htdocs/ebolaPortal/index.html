UCSC Ebola Portal Genomic resources related to the 2014 West Africa outbreak

About

The 2014 Ebola epidemic in West Africa has stirred international response and renewed efforts to develop effective preventative and treatment options. In response to a request for help from vaccine researchers, we have fast-tracked the UCSC Ebola Genome Browser built with viral sequences from previous outbreaks as well as the 2014 outbreak. This site also provides related tools and information that can be used to further the understanding of Ebola.

Ebola Biology

Ebola is a viral disease that effects humans and other primates. The symptoms start within three weeks and include fever, sore throat, muscle pain and headaches. Vomiting, diarrhea, as well as decreased function of the liver and kidneys follow. At this point the virus causes both internal and external bleeding. The entire cycle takes two to four weeks. The virus is highly virulent, with survival rates as low as ten percent. Additionally it is highly contagious, spreading through bodily fluids and from corpses of the victims.

The life cycle starts with a copy of the negative strand of RNA packaged into a viral capsule. This capsule crashes into a cell and gains entry. Immediately it activates the viral polymerase to copy the negative strand into positive strands. Then the cell's machinery is used to read the positive strand into five proteins. Two of these proteins are cut to create a total of seven proteins. After a required level of proteins is achieved the virus switches to a replication mode. Here it transcribes the positive strand back into negative copies and packages them into capsule which are released to start the cycle again.
