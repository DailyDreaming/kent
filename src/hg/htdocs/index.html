News

The April 1, 2001 assembly is now up. The sequencing centers were very busy adding depth to draft clones and finishing between December and April. The result is a larger fraction of the genome covered and substantially larger contigs in the part that is covered. We're aiming for a new assembly approximately every two months.

There is a browser with a fairly basic set of tracks available for the April 2001 assembly. We'll be adding more tracks over time. Currently we only have two machines capable of acting as BLAT servers. We've had to remove BLAT from the October 2000 assembly to accommodate April 2001. We should be getting a new machine in a week or two, and will restore the October 2000 BLAT service at that point.

About the Working Draft

This page contains links to an assembly of the current draft of the human genome. The human genome is approximately 3.2 billion bases. More than 90% of the genome has been sequenced by the International Human Genome Project, and approximately 89% is included in the Dec. 12, 2000 draft. This draft genome is composed of hundreds of thousands of fragments of various sizes. The order and orientation of the fragments is often not known from the sequencing process itself. In some cases the same part of the genome will be duplicated in more than one fragment.

This assembly is an attempt to merge together overlapping fragments, and to order and orient non-overlapping fragments based on mRNA, EST, paired plasmid reads, and other information. The ordering and orientation is not perfect, yet it still should be useful to many researchers. Much of the redundancy found in the human genomic clones submitted to the public databases GenBank, EMBL, and DDBJ is removed in this working draft. The average sequence contig size is substantially larger in it than in the records for individual clones.

The data given here are described more fully in the paper Initial Sequencing and Analysis of the Human Genome by the International Human Genome Sequencing Consortium, Nature, 409: 860-921, Feb. 2001.
