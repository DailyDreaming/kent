  About the Working Draft

This site contains a working draft of the human genome, which is over 90% complete. Approximately half of the sequence is in a highly accurate 'finished' state. The other half is merely 'draft' quality. Some care must be taken interpreting draft regions, but these are still often very useful to the working scientist. We encourage you to explore the working draft with the genome browser, which displays the work of many annotators worldwide.



  News

A preliminary assembly of the August 2001 freeze is now available in the genome browser. This assembly is a major improvement over the April 2001 freeze assembly. Though we at UCSC played a role in this the major credit belongs to the finishing groups, who now have removed gaps and misassemblies from clones spanning over half of the human genome; to the mapping groups who picked clones well into many difficult regions, and separated tough large scale duplications; and to Imre Vastrick and colleagues at Ensembl who devised a program that effectively merges the best features of the Wash U genome-wide map and the chromosome-by-chromosome maps from the various sequencing centers. Our assembly of the human genome is still not perfect of course, but it's come an awfully long way since the first working draft assembly June 22, 2000.

This assembly has successfully passed our internal quality control measures. We will release the sequence and annotations in bulk downloadable form in a week or so after the external testers have had a chance to further verify it. Meanwhile if you notice any systematic problems please let us know at genome@cse.ucsc.edu. Though the state of the working draft has improved considerably in the since the April 2001 freeze if you see marks in the 'gap' track, particularly solid marks, the relative order and orientation is still uncertain. In some cases do to the complex repeat structure of the genome the assembly will be off even in the absence of gaps. Localized errors of this sort are corrected during the finishing procedure. There is not much we can do about them here at UC Santa Cruz. However if you notice any large scale systematic problems, these may be due to an undetected error in our procedure. Please do report any such large scale problems you detect.

The tracks available on the August 2001 browser are quite limited at the moment. More tracks will as usual show up over time.
